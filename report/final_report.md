# Remaining Useful Life Prediction of Turbofan Engines Using Multi-Sensor Time-Series Data

---

## 1. Introduction

Jet engine maintenance is an arms race against physics. Every flight cycle subjects turbine blades to thermal fatigue, compressor coatings to erosion, and bearings to cumulative wear. The industry response has historically been conservative: replace parts on a fixed schedule, regardless of their actual condition. This keeps planes flying safely, but at enormous cost — airlines routinely discard components with 30–50% of their useful life remaining.

The alternative — **condition-based predictive maintenance** — requires solving a specific technical problem: given a stream of in-situ sensor measurements, predict how many more operational cycles a component can safely endure. This is the **Remaining Useful Life (RUL) prediction** problem.

This project implements a complete RUL prediction pipeline for turbofan engines. The system ingests raw multi-sensor time-series data, transforms it into fixed-length sequences, passes those sequences through a hybrid CNN-BiLSTM-Attention model, and outputs both a point prediction and a calibrated uncertainty interval via Monte Carlo Dropout. The pipeline is designed for reproducibility, deployment, and extension — not just as a one-off experiment.

---

## 2. Problem Statement

**Input:** A multivariate time series of 21 sensor readings and 3 operational settings, sampled once per operating cycle for a single turbofan engine.

**Output:** A scalar prediction of the number of remaining cycles before the engine reaches a failure state, accompanied by an uncertainty estimate.

**Constraints:**
- The model must handle variable-length engine histories (some engines have operated for 30 cycles, others for 300) through a fixed-width sliding window.
- Uncertainty quantification is mandatory — a maintenance decision based on a point prediction alone is unsafe.
- The system must not leak future information into past predictions (engine-level train/val splitting).

**What makes this hard:** Degradation is not a step function. It accumulates gradually, obscured by sensor noise and operating-condition variability. Two engines at the same chronological age can have vastly different remaining lifespans depending on their individual degradation trajectories. The model must learn to read the *slope* of degradation, not just its current level.

---

## 3. Dataset

### 3.1 Source and Provenance

The **Commercial Modular Aero-Propulsion System Simulation (C-MAPSS)** dataset was generated by NASA's Prognostics Center of Excellence using a high-fidelity thermodynamic simulation of a turbofan engine. It has become the standard benchmark for prognostics research, appearing in 500+ peer-reviewed publications.

### 3.2 FD001 Subset

| Property | Value | Implication |
|---|---|---|
| Training engines | 100 (run-to-failure) | Complete degradation curves available |
| Test engines | 100 (cut-off) | Must predict from partial history |
| Operating conditions | 1 | No confounding from operational regime |
| Fault modes | 1 (HPC degradation) | Isolates the modeling problem from multi-fault disambiguation |
| Sensor channels | 21 | Redundancy: ~7 are flat, ~14 carry signal |
| Settings | 3 | Effectively constant in FD001 |
| Total training rows | 20,631 | Modest by deep learning standards |

### 3.3 What the Data Actually Looks Like

Each engine starts healthy and degrades monotonically until failure. The training set captures the *complete* degradation arc; the test set is truncated at an arbitrary point, and the task is to predict how much arc remains.

**Key observation from EDA:** Engine lifespans range from 128 to 362 cycles (median ~200). This 3× spread means the model cannot rely on chronological age alone — it must learn degradation signatures from sensor trajectories.

## 4. Data Preprocessing

### 4.1 RUL Target Engineering

The raw target (cycles until failure) decreases linearly from the engine's first cycle to its last. We apply a **piecewise-linear cap at 125 cycles**:

```
RUL(t) = min(max_cycle - current_cycle, 125)
```

**Why 125?** Empirically, sensor readings for engines with RUL > 125 are statistically indistinguishable. The cap converts an impossible regression problem (distinguishing RUL=200 from RUL=300) into a tractable one (distinguishing RUL=50 from RUL=100). This is not a free parameter — the literature consensus (Saxena et al., Heimes et al.) converges on 120–130.

### 4.2 Feature Selection

Seven sensors (1, 5, 6, 10, 16, 18, 19) have coefficient of variation < 0.01 across all engines — they are essentially constant. Retaining them adds noise without signal. One setting (setting_3) has zero variance. After removal, **16 features** remain.

### 4.3 Normalization Strategy

**MinMaxScaler**, fitted exclusively on training data. The scaler is serialized to disk (`data/processed/scaler.pkl`) so that test evaluation, Streamlit inference, and FastAPI deployment all apply identical transformations — a detail that silently breaks deployed models when ignored.

### 4.4 Sequence Generation

A sliding window of length 30 moves across each engine's history, producing (window, RUL) pairs. For early cycles where the engine has fewer than 30 historical steps, we left-pad with zeros. This mirrors the deployment scenario: a newly installed engine has limited history, and the model should handle this gracefully rather than refusing to predict.

## 5. Model Architecture

### 5.1 Design Philosophy

RUL prediction from sensor time-series requires three distinct capabilities:

| Capability | Why it matters | Which component provides it |
|---|---|---|
| **Local pattern extraction** | A sudden temperature spike at cycle 28 carries different meaning than a gradual rise | 1D-CNN |
| **Long-range sequential memory** | Degradation accumulates over tens of cycles; early trends inform late predictions | BiLSTM |
| **Adaptive time-step weighting** | The final cycles in the window are typically more informative than the first | Multi-head self-attention |

No single architecture handles all three efficiently. The hybrid design composes them.

### 5.2 Architecture Specification

```
Input: (batch, 30, 16)
  │
  ├─ Conv1D(16→64, kernel=3, pad=1) + BatchNorm + ReLU + Dropout(0.3)
  ├─ Conv1D(64→64, kernel=3, pad=1) + BatchNorm + ReLU + Dropout(0.3)
  │
  ├─ BiLSTM(input=64, hidden=128, layers=2, dropout=0.3)
  │   → output: (batch, 30, 256)
  │
  ├─ MultiheadAttention(embed=256, heads=4, dropout=0.3)
  ├─ Residual connection + LayerNorm
  │   → take last timestep: (batch, 256)
  │
  ├─ FC(256→64) + ReLU + Dropout(0.3)
  └─ FC(64→1) → scalar RUL
```

**Total parameters: 889,857.** Intentionally compact — overfitting is a real risk with only 100 training engines.

### 5.3 Transformer Variant

An alternative architecture (`TransformerRUL`) replaces the BiLSTM with a 4-layer Transformer encoder using learned positional encodings, pre-norm, GELU activations, and global average pooling. This variant is fully compatible with the existing dataset pipeline and can be trained via `python -m src.train --model transformer`.

### 5.4 Dropout as an Inference Tool

Dropout (p=0.3) is applied at five points in the architecture: CNN blocks, LSTM output, attention, and FC layers. At training time, this is standard regularization. At inference time, enabling dropout and running N forward passes transforms the deterministic model into an approximate Bayesian neural network (Gal & Ghahramani, 2016). The variance across passes estimates epistemic uncertainty — uncertainty due to limited training data, not sensor noise.

## 6. Training Protocol

| Setting | Value | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Optimizer | Adam | Adaptive learning rates for heterogeneous parameters |
| Learning rate | 1e-3 | Standard for Adam on small-medium models |
| Weight decay | 1e-5 | Light L2 regularization; heavier values degrade LSTM training |
| LR schedule | ReduceLROnPlateau (factor=0.5, patience=5) | Adapts to loss plateau without manual tuning |
| Gradient clipping | max_norm=1.0 | Prevents exploding gradients in the LSTM |
| Batch size | 256 | Balances gradient noise with memory constraints |
| Early stopping | Patience=12 epochs | Prevents overfitting; typical runs stop at epoch 20–40 |
| Train/val split | 80/20 by engine (not by row) | Prevents temporal data leakage |

**Key design choice:** We split by *engine*, not by *time step*. If rows from the same engine appeared in both train and validation sets, the model would learn to interpolate within a known degradation curve rather than generalize to unseen engines. This is the most common data leakage mistake in time-series ML.

## 7. Evaluation

### 7.1 Metrics

| Metric | Value | Interpretation |
|---|---|---|
| **RMSE** | 15.11 | The model's predictions are off by ~15 cycles RMS. Penalizes large errors heavily. |
| **MAE** | 12.07 | Average absolute error of ~12 cycles. More robust to outliers than RMSE. |
| **NASA Score** | 438.59 | Asymmetric penalty function (late predictions penalized harder). Lower is better. |
| **MC Dropout RMSE** | 15.30 | Aggregating 50 stochastic forward passes barely increases error — the uncertainty estimate is "free." |
| **Mean σ** | ±10.51 | Average 68% confidence band width. Well-calibrated: engines near failure get ±5, healthy engines get ±15. |

### 7.2 Benchmark Context

Published FD001 results span a wide range:

| Method | RMSE | Source |
|---|---|---|
| Linear regression baseline | ~25–30 | Saxena et al. (2008) |
| Standard LSTM | 16–18 | Zheng et al. (2017) |
| CNN-LSTM hybrids | 13–16 | Li et al. (2018) |
| Deep ensemble methods | 11–13 | Various (2019+) |
| **This project** | **15.11** | CNN-BiLSTM-Attention |

Our result sits within the competitive range. The remaining gap to state-of-the-art (~12) is attributable to: (a) single model vs. ensemble, (b) symmetric MSE loss vs. asymmetric NASA scoring, and (c) no dataset-specific tuning. These are deliberate choices — the project prioritizes clean generalization and uncertainty awareness over benchmark optimization.

### 7.3 Where the Model Succeeds and Fails

**Succeeds:** Engines deep into degradation (RUL < 50). The model tracks the steep decline in sensor readings accurately, with tight confidence intervals.

**Struggles:** Engines near the RUL cap boundary (RUL ~100–125). The transition from "flat" to "degrading" is subtle, and the model's uncertainty correctly widens in this regime.

### 7.4 NASA Scoring Function

The NASA scoring function applies asymmetric penalties:

```
d = predicted - actual
score = exp(-d/13) - 1    if d < 0  (late prediction → dangerous)
score = exp(d/10) - 1     if d ≥ 0  (early prediction → wasteful)
```

This metric is included in the evaluation output but not used for training. Using it as a training loss would bias the model toward over-predicting RUL (earlier maintenance scheduling), which is operationally conservative but statistically suboptimal for learning the true degradation function.

## 8. Uncertainty Analysis

### 8.1 MC Dropout Mechanics

At inference, we run the model 50 times with dropout active. Each pass samples a different subnetwork, producing a slightly different prediction. The mean of these predictions is the point estimate; the standard deviation is the uncertainty.

### 8.2 Calibration

The uncertainty calibration plot shows that engines for which the model reports higher uncertainty *do* tend to have larger absolute errors. This is the key property of a useful uncertainty estimate — it's not just noise, it correlates with actual prediction difficulty.

### 8.3 Practical Interpretation

For a maintenance engineer, the output looks like:

> *Engine #42: predicted RUL = 35 cycles (95% CI: 14–56)*

The wide interval flags this engine for closer monitoring. Compare with:

> *Engine #17: predicted RUL = 8 cycles (95% CI: 3–13)*

The tight interval and low RUL trigger an immediate maintenance order.

A production-ready REST API (`api/main.py`) exposes three endpoints:

| Endpoint | Method | Purpose |
|---|---|---|

## 10. Challenges and Reflections

**The sequence length dilemma.** A 30-cycle window is a guess. Too short, and the model can't see long-range trends. Too long, and early (uninformative) cycles dilute the signal. I chose 30 because it captures the steep-degradation phase for most engines without excessive padding for short-lived ones. A proper solution would be either hyperparameter search or an architecture that handles variable-length inputs natively (padded Transformer).

**Overfitting pressure.** 100 engines is not a lot of data for a model with ~900K parameters. The combination of dropout, weight decay, early stopping, and engine-level splitting keeps overfitting in check, but the model would clearly benefit from FD002–FD004 data augmentation.

**The cap threshold question.** 125 is borrowed from the literature. I tested 100 and 150; 125 gave the best validation loss. A more principled approach would be a learned soft-cap (sigmoid transition) rather than a hard clip.

---

## 11. Conclusion

This project demonstrates that a moderately sized hybrid architecture — CNN for local features, BiLSTM for sequential memory, attention for adaptive weighting — can achieve competitive RUL prediction on FD001 while providing well-calibrated uncertainty estimates at near-zero additional cost.

The engineering contributions are as important as the modeling ones: engine-level data splitting prevents leakage, scaler persistence ensures deployment consistency, and the modular codebase separates data, model, training, and serving concerns cleanly enough that swapping in a Transformer backbone requires changing one command-line flag.

Whether the system is "good enough" for production depends on the maintenance context. An RMSE of 15 cycles with ±10 uncertainty is insufficient for safety-critical go/no-go decisions but highly valuable for scheduling optimization — moving from calendar-based to condition-based maintenance scheduling, where even coarse predictions yield 20–30% reductions in unplanned downtime.

## 12. Future Work

**Immediate priorities:**
- Extend to FD002–FD004 to validate generalization across operating conditions and fault modes.
- Integrate SHAP or Integrated Gradients for per-sensor, per-timestep attribution — critical for maintenance engineer trust.

**Medium-term:**
- Implement asymmetric loss training (NASA scoring function) as an optional training mode to align optimization with operational risk.
- Build a real-time streaming inference pipeline (Kafka → model → dashboard) for live engine telemetry.

**Research directions:**
- Heteroscedastic output (separate epistemic and aleatoric uncertainty).
- Transfer learning: pre-train on C-MAPSS simulation, fine-tune on real engine telemetry.
- Neural ODE-based continuous-time degradation models that don't require fixed-width windows.
